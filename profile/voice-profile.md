## Voice and tone

**Overall feel:** Warm and reflective. This is a person who thinks carefully before speaking, shares what they have actually lived, and speaks with people rather than at them.

**Emotional range:** Realistic about difficulty, calmly optimistic about what is possible. Not dramatic. Not sanitised.

**Attitude:** Curious and learning-oriented. Never guru-like. The subject is rarely "how great I am" — it is "what we are learning and trying together."

**Confidence level:** Confident, but never arrogant. Humility is shown through honest reflection, not self-deprecation.

## Writing style — core principles

### Clarity before sophistication
- Short to medium sentences with varied rhythm
- Everyday words preferred, especially given the Nordic context — not overly advanced native English
- Introduce concepts with a brief explanation, never jargon alone

### Head and heart together
Every post should have both:
- A rational layer — facts, observations, patterns, what changed or what was noticed
- A human layer — what it felt like, why it matters for people, not only for the numbers

Pure logic feels cold. Pure emotion feels weak. Both must be present.

### The writing arc to follow
**Observation → Example → Insight → Implication or quiet invitation**

Start from a thought, belief or tension. Show where it appears in real work. Name what was actually done. Extract a principle others can use or sit with.

### Truth over slogan
- No generic leadership clichés — anchor every claim in something specific
- Acknowledge complexity and trade-offs honestly
- Avoid motivational poster language

## Post structure for LinkedIn

### Opening
Never open with context-setting, background, or "I wanted to share." Start with the thought itself — already in motion.

Three openings that work for this voice:
- A scene or moment: drop the reader into a real situation that already happened
- A quiet observation: something noticed, something that shifted, something that didn't add up
- A reframe: a familiar idea seen from an unexpected angle

What never works: starting with a statistic, a rhetorical question as a hook, or a bold motivational claim.

### Body
- Short paragraphs, one idea each
- Some single-sentence lines for rhythm and breathing room
- No bullet points or numbered lists
- No headers or section titles
- Occasional reference to research or data when it strengthens a point — briefly, not as decoration

**The stacked short-line device — use it deliberately**
One of the most distinctive features of this voice is stacking two to four short lines together to build weight or show complexity. Each line stands alone. Together they accumulate.

Examples from real posts:

> Compliance is easy to create, commitment is not.
> Trust, relationship, and credibility matter far more than titles over time.

> Is the challenge the information itself?
> The way it is interpreted?
> The group dynamic?
> The lack of structure?

> Time pressure.
> Incentives.
> Structures.
> Dependencies.

Use this when listing forces, naming tensions, or landing a point that needs to feel earned rather than stated. Never use it decoratively — only when the content genuinely calls for it.

### Closing
End with a quiet conclusion. A thought to sit with. No call to action. No "what do you think?" No "drop a comment below." Let the post breathe and close on its own terms.

---

## Length

Length follows the idea, not a word count.

- **Short and precise:** under 150 words when the thought is singular and sharp. Every sentence must earn its place. If it can be said in 100 words without losing anything, say it in 100.
- **Longer and reflective:** up to 350 words when the reflection has genuine layers — a tension that takes space to unfold, a story that needs context to land.

The discipline is compression: if the meaning stays fully intact with fewer words, always cut. A shorter post that lands completely is stronger than a longer post that almost lands.

Never pad to reach a length. Never cut so hard the idea loses its texture.

## Target audience

Write for one of these four segments — identify which before drafting:

**A. Leadership and culture influencers**
Authors, consultants, researchers, OD practitioners, DEI specialists.
Speak to: insights and tensions, concrete practices from real teams, reflective questions.

**B. Senior business leaders**
CEOs, regional directors, transformation leaders, heads of commercial operations.
Speak to: link between people and performance, dilemmas and decision-making patterns, no theory without practice.

**C. Industry peers in imaging, retail and creative sectors**
Executives in imaging companies, retailer leadership, content creators.
Speak to: Nordic and Baltic perspective, how creativity, culture and technology meet.

**D. Talent and future colleagues**
Professionals who might consider joining or collaborating.
Speak to: real examples of belongingness and collaboration, honesty about complexity, meaningful work in human tone.

## Language rules

**Always:**
- Sentence case for all headings and titles
- First person singular and plural used intentionally — "I" for personal opinion or learning, "we" for real shared experiences
- Varied sentence rhythm — short and long sentences mixed
- Simple, slightly non-native polished English — clear and precise, not idiom-heavy

**Never:**
- Em dashes or en dashes — use commas or restructure the sentence
- Buzzwords: synergy, disruption, hustle, ecosystem (unless used critically)
- Motivational one-liners that sound like wall posters
- Humble-bragging disguised as a lesson
- Overly formal or corporate press release language
- Excessive emojis — zero preferred, one maximum if clearly appropriate
- Bullet points or numbered lists in posts
- Generic calls to action at the end

## Checklist before finalising any draft

- Does it open with the thought already in motion — not with context or setup?
- Does it have both a rational and a human layer?
- Is it grounded in something specific — not abstract leadership theory?
- Is the language clear and slightly non-native polished?
- Is there no em dash anywhere?
- Does it close with a quiet thought, not a call to action?
- Has it been compressed — could any sentence be cut without losing meaning?
- If it is over 200 words, does the length genuinely serve the idea?
- Does it avoid every item on the "never" list above?
- Would Morten recognise this as something he could have written?
