## Post 1 — ~2 months ago | 1,250 impressions | 21 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Illusionary alignment / sense-making / leadership behaviour

The quiet leadership mistake behind illusionary alignment.

My experience is that the problem in many leadership conversations is rarely that people disagree with the data.

It is that we mistake shared information for shared understanding.

In periods of planning or decision making, we often bring many signals into the room at once. Performance indicators. Trends. Benchmarks. Comparisons. Forecasts.

Everyone looks at the same material. And still, people leave with different stories in their heads.

Illusionary alignment emerges not because anyone is wrong, but because meaning is compressed too early. Multiple interpretations exist, yet structure arrives before those differences have had space to surface.

As leaders, the instinct is understandable. We want clarity. We want momentum. We summarise, prioritise, and frame the situation.

Sometimes that is exactly what the room needs.

Sometimes it is not.

I have learned that when I enter these conversations, I am rarely the subject matter expert. My real responsibility is to read the room.

Is the challenge the information itself? The way it is interpreted? The group dynamic? The lack of structure? Or the absence of a clear decision?

I have learned, sometimes the hard way, that the same leadership move that creates progress in one moment can quietly shut learning down in another.

There are moments where dialogue matters more than direction. Moments where structure helps. And moments where leadership means making a call, even without full alignment.

The tension I keep working with is knowing when the room needs more sense making, and when it needs a decision.

Getting that wrong is easy. Getting it right is leadership.

## Post 2 — ~2 months ago | 1,057 impressions | 23 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Systems thinking / leadership influence / shaping conditions

I have started to notice that as leaders, we often believe we are steering behavior, when we may actually be revealing the limits of our influence.

This usually shows up in uncomfortable moments. When the intention was clear. The agreement was real. And the outcome still does not match what we expected.

Our first reflex is understandable.

We look for misalignment. We look at execution that failed. We assume that somewhere, someone chose differently.

But people do not act in isolation. They act inside constraints.

Time pressure. Incentives. Structures. Dependencies. Informal signals that matter more than the formal ones. Trade-offs built into roles and priorities.

These forces shape behavior far more reliably than intention ever will.

So when outcomes surprise us, it may not be because people resisted or misunderstood the goal. It may be because the system made certain behaviors more likely than others, regardless of what anyone wanted.

That realisation has changed how I think about leadership.

Not so much about directing people, but more about shaping the conditions they work within.

Not all constraints can, or should, be removed. Some are essential for focus, coordination, and trust. But many remain invisible or unexamined, until results force us to look at them.

When outcomes surprise us, we should ask ourselves: What are we actually learning about the system we are leading?

## Post 3 — ~4 months ago | 2,177 impressions | 72 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Planning / diversity of thought / collective intelligence

We have just finished our Activation Planning Workshop for next year. On the surface it is planning. In reality it is a test of how we think and work.

Many organisations treat planning as a technical exercise. Gather numbers, align tasks, lock the plan. That creates movement, but not necessarily clarity or commitment.

We take a different approach.

Each of the four groups begins by looking at the different strengths, experiences and thinking styles around the table. It reminds us that we do not only interpret data differently, we interpret the world differently, and turning those differences into better decisions is a deliberate part of the process.

We interview customers and partners before we draw conclusions internally. And when we finally meet, the goal is not fast alignment or quick decisions, but the time and space to talk, challenge and build a shared proposal based on insight.

The point is clear.

Strong plans come from combining evidence with genuine diversity of thought. If everyone thinks alike, you get speed. You rarely get truth or original ideas.

This is why performance and culture cannot be treated as two tracks. The strength of the plan comes from insight and the way people challenge and build on it together.

Now the process continues as ideas grow, sharpen and move into next steps.

## Post 4 — ~4 months ago | 2,105 impressions | 45 reactions | 1 comment | 1 repost
Theme: Belongingness / Anne-Marie Finch book launch / collective superpowers

One belief was reinforced for me this week: Belongingness is not soft. Leading for belongingness is one of the hardest disciplines in leadership because it forces you to lead without hiding behind hierarchy, process or corporate language.

On Tuesday, I joined the launch of Anne-Marie Finch's new book "Kollektive Superkræfter" (Collective Superpowers). Her work has shaped our journey in Canon Nordic and Baltic for years.

True Self taught us that performance scales when people use their real strengths. Team Discovery added the collective layer, the moment we stop trying to make people alike and start understanding how differences multiply value.

Across eight countries, this is not theory. You cannot rely on proximity or titles to make things work. You rely on trust, clarity and the courage to let people show up as they are. When that happens, strategy stops being a document and becomes lived behaviour.

So yes, it is an honour to be included as a case in the book. But the real value is the mindset behind it, the mindset that lets us build one culture from different voices without forcing everyone to be the same.

Congratulations Anne-Marie Finch on the launch, and thank you for continuing to challenge how we lead.

## Post 5 — ~4 months ago | 2,051 impressions | 62 reactions | 3 comments
Theme: Workshop that slipped / blind spots / team culture revealed in imperfect moments

Every leader knows the moment when a plan no longer fits the room, and I met one of those last week.

The first half of our workshop worked. Then we reached the final exercise, and I could see within minutes that the way I had framed the foundation did not support the discussion we needed.

I had noticed small signals earlier, but I trusted that the structure would hold once we got there. In the moment, watching the team work with a framework that was slipping, my first reaction was frustration with myself. I kept the exercise going because, in the moment, I felt stopping it would have created more confusion, but at the same time, I felt I had missed the right moment to adjust.

A night of distance made the picture clearer.

The issue was not the strategy or the exercise. It was the hesitation I had pushed aside along the way. Blind spots rarely arrive with volume. They appear as quiet signals we override when momentum feels good or when we hope things will align if we just keep moving.

What stayed with me even more was how the team responded afterwards. No defensiveness. No blame. Just steady, practical engagement, clarifying questions and a genuine effort to repair the gap. That small moment revealed more about our culture than any polished session could.

Looking back, the moment felt less like a failure and more like a reminder.

Progress depends on noticing the quiet signals before they turn into gaps. A team's culture becomes clearest in the moments that are not planned, polished or perfect.

## Post 6 — ~2 months ago | 1,627 impressions | 27 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Power / trust / leadership as carrying power consciously

Trust does not remove power from organisations. It changes where it sits, and how it is exercised.

Even the decision to delegate power, to distribute authority, or to step back from formal roles, is itself an expression of power.

I have been reflecting on leadership, power, and trust, and on a tension I cannot quite let go of.

Leadership built only on formal authority is fragile. Compliance is easy to create, commitment is not. Trust, relationship, and credibility matter far more than titles over time.

But power is still there.

Even with good intentions. Even in flat structures or distributed leadership. Even when we genuinely want to do things differently.

Power lives in roles, in decision rights, in attention, in tempo, and in who sets priorities. It also lives in who gets to design the system in the first place, a privilege not all leaders have.

Many leaders operate inside structures they did not create, under pressure that does not pause to allow time to examine the operating system they are part of.

So the real work of leadership is not to deny power, but to carry it consciously.

Not because power is a right, but because it is a condition that comes with responsibility.

One practical place to start is to notice where power quietly shows up, in how fast we decide, who gets the last word, and whose perspectives are invited in.

Trust grows when power is used with restraint, clarity, and respect, in service of something shared and worth committing to.

Leadership is not about stepping out of power. It is about staying human while standing inside it.

## Post 7 — ~3 months ago | 1,529 impressions | 49 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: New year / cycles / carrying forward rather than reinventing

There is something I genuinely enjoy about the turn of a year. Not that I am a big fan of "new year, new me".

Over time, I have come to appreciate that life moves in cycles, and that time passes whether we notice it or not.

What matters to me is not leaving the old behind, but carrying it forward. The experiences, relationships, and lessons from the past year do not disappear on January 1st. They become the foundation for the next cycle. Quietly shaping both what we build and how we show up for each other.

If we never pause to notice these moments, life has a tendency to slip through our fingers. Not dramatically. Just gradually.

So here's to 2026. Not as a reinvention, but as the beginning of a new cycle, grounded in everything the previous one gave us.

## Post 8 — ~4 months ago | 1,231 impressions | 34 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Magnus Wennman / photojournalism / Canon storytelling / children in conflict

A child falling asleep safely. We all know that moment so well, yet for many children it is not reality.

That thought stayed with me after our internal Nordic/Baltic Monday Morning Meeting last week, where we invited photojournalist and Canon Ambassador Magnus Wennman to share some of his work.

Magnus has spent 25 years documenting people whose lives look nothing like ours, often meeting children in situations where safety is not a given but a distant hope.

Hearing him describe the contrast between his own son's effortless sleep and the children he meets in conflict zones hit me deeply as a father of two.

It connected something familiar with something almost impossible to imagine.

What makes his work so powerful is how he shows reality without adding weight. No drama, no staging, no attempt to steer your reaction. Just an honest, human look at people living through situations far outside our daily experience.

We bring voices like his into our organisation for a reason.

They remind us that our work is not only about performance and budgets. It is also about enabling stories that matter and supporting the people who use our tools to show the world as it is.

## Post 9 — ~4 months ago | 776 impressions | 30 reactions | 1 comment
Theme: Simon Lereng Wilmont / filmmaker / hope as a force / Canon storytelling

Stories can help us to care. They can keep hope alive. And once in a while, we meet the people who dedicate their lives to telling them.

Last week our Nordic/Baltic team had one of those moments.

Every few weeks we invite external guests into our Nordic and Baltic Monday Morning Meeting to broaden our perspective.

This time we were joined by filmmaker Simon Lereng Wilmont, known for the Oscar-nominated A House Made of Splinters, a documentary shaped by real lives, real loss, and the quiet strength of children living close to the war in Ukraine.

Simon reminded us that the stories are already out there. Our role, when we can, is simply to help shine a light on them.

Among his reflections, one point stayed with me: hope is not optional.

It is a necessary condition for the people in his films to endure their situation.

And it is necessary for us as viewers: Without any hope visible, most might turn away. When there is just a glimpse of hope, we stay open, we care, and we are more likely to act.

Our contribution is small in the bigger picture, but still full of meaning. When our products enable someone like Simon to document humanity, dignity and resilience, we play a quiet part in bringing these stories into the world.

## Post 10 — ~3 months ago | 701 impressions | 30 reactions
Theme: Employee survey / survey as lens not verdict / measurement and culture

We recently completed our employee survey. And once again, it made me think less about the results themselves, and more about how we use them.

I am glad we do them. I also try to be thoughtful about what I use them for.

In many organisations, and sometimes closer to home than we like to admit, surveys quietly become evaluations.

A score to explain. A delta to compare. Something to defend or fix. That framing gives clarity, but it also closes the conversation surprisingly fast.

I have started to see surveys differently over time.

Less as an answer, more as a lens.

A lens does not tell you what is true. It shapes how you look.

It helps you notice where alignment feels strong, and where it feels fragile.

And where there may be a gap between what we aspire to be, what is important to people, and how it is experienced in everyday work.

And sometimes, it raises questions that are harder to answer with a score.

Of course, the scores are interesting. I look at them. I care about them. And yes, I want them to be high. Higher scores usually mean people feel we are doing a better job, and that matters.

But the number itself matters less than what it invites us to talk about next.

For me, that is where the real value of a survey lies. Not only whether we moved up or down, but why. What we are proud of, what we want to be more intentional about, and what we would like to be special about us.

In a KPI-driven world, I believe that distinction is important.

Not everything that counts becomes clearer when we measure it harder. Some things only emerge when we slow down and talk about what kind of team we want to be.

What is right will always depend on context, people, and ambition. But treating signals as invitations rather than verdicts has helped us create more ownership, more honesty, and better conversations.

If a survey helps us do that, then for me, it has already moved us forward.
